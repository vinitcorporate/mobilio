tHE STORY OF PAITKAR ART
We take pride in preserving Paitkar paintings, one of India’s oldest tribal art forms, originating from Amadubi village in Jharkhand’s East Singhbhum district. Practiced by the Chitrakar community, this unique scroll painting tradition is often called the “Scroll Paintings of Jharkhand” due to its narrative style.

Our artists, known as Paitkars or Chitrakars, create hand-painted scrolls that depict mythological tales, tribal folklore, and social themes. This centuries-old tradition keeps the oral and visual history of tribal India alive, passed down through generations.

Through this platform, we are committed to reviving, promoting, and sustaining this rich artistic heritage, ensuring that the legacy of Paitkar painting continues to thrive.

Hands that weave stories

Amit Chitrakar
Anil Chitrakar, a 71-year-old artisan from Amadubi village, is a master of the traditional Paitkar painting. Despite his passion and dedication, Anil faces challenges as the art form struggles to sustain livelihoods. Taught by his father, he learned the craft using natural materials like leaves, stones, and goat hair. However, the lack of a platform to sell his work and dwindling interest among the younger generation have pushed many to alternative jobs. Anil, now nearly blind, relies on a meager pension and occasional singing for income. His story underscores the urgent need for support to preserve this ancient art.
Vijay Chitrakar He has worked for around 33 years in this particular field and he is a very renowed artist in the village. He mostly works as a commercial artist who wants to make profit out of his art. He affirms that foreigners are his main customers as they understand the nuances of his art and appreciate the use of natural resources such as organic colours to perform this craft. He believes that people in India do not understand importance of this art as they are mostly ignorant about their culture and they are not keen to learn more about this form of art. Although he sells these crafts with the ulterior motive of earning profit, he said that it is not very profitable for him and he doesn't earn exorbitant amounts of money from the sale of such craft and he is somehow surviving
in this world where there are not many takers for this form of art.
KISHORE GAYEN
When he was 9 years old he learned the craft from his grandfather despite the discontent of his father who wanted him to pursue a career where he has a secure job but his grandfather wanted him to learn this craft so that it remains relevant, but he had studied fine arts so he believed that continuation of the practice of this craft was his destiny.
He has conducted many informative workshops in Kolkata to spread awareness regarding the craft and also to teach people how to practice this craft. He also has acquired tenders floated by the government authorities to create street art and utilise the craft in renovating the government buildings and making them look better. He sells his artwork for profit to keep the craft alive and also for his sustenance. He lives on a property inherently owned by the government so it is very uncertain whether he will always have a roof above his head and be able to live his life comfortably.
